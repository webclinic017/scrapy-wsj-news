George Makari’s concern with xenophobia goes back to a childhood trauma. In 1974, at the age of 13, he was taken on a family visit to his parents’ native Beirut. Suddenly, the travelers found themselves caught in the midst of what would become a civil war. “To me, it was bizarre,” Dr. Makari recalls in “Of Fear and Strangers: A History of Xenophobia.” He continues: “All these bewildering sects were far more alike than different. All were Levantines who spoke the same dialect; all loved the same punning humor, devoured the same cuisine, abided by strict rules of hospitality, and approached any purchase as a three-act play: bargain, stage a walk-out, then settle. They were quick with proverbs and went agog when Fairuz sang. And yet, subtle distinctions in their identities now meant life or death.”
Today, Dr. Makari, a psychiatrist, psychoanalyst and the director of Weill Cornell’s DeWitt Wallace Institute of Psychiatry, sees xenophobia as a threat to social peace, not only in the Middle East but also in Europe and North America, where recent political convulsions have been driven by a bristling hostility toward strangers and outsiders. Dr. Makari is clear that a lot of different impulses are often conflated here: “ethnocentrism, ultranationalism, racism, misogyny, sexism, anti-Semitism, homophobia, transphobia, or Islamophobia.” What might they have in common? “Is there any one term specific enough to not be meaningless, while broad enough to allow us to consider whatever common strands exist between these phenomena?” He thinks that there is: xenophobia. And if all these disorders are variants of the same affliction, then perhaps they have the same cause and might be susceptible to the same treatment.

2021-10-22 09:52:00 Books & Arts Fiction: Elizabeth Strout’s ‘Oh William!’ The heroine of ‘My Name Is Lucy Barton’ returns in a story of trauma and compassion. So central and inalienable is the role of trauma in the present-day understanding of personhood that fiction on the subject often seems flatfooted. No longer is it sufficient for a novel simply to reveal a character’s traumatic past, since the past is now presumed to be a place of emotional and physical damage; nor is it enough merely to chart a character’s recovery, since “recovering” and “living” are virtually synonymous in usage. Today a deeper engagement is required. Hanya Yanagihara’s “A Little Life” caused shock waves by pushing child-abuse narratives to their logical—or perhaps absurdist—end point. And in a quieter but no less comprehensive fashion, Elizabeth’s Strout’s Lucy Barton novels, of which “Oh William!” is the latest, have become essential to the contemporary canon by taking a wider view on the interpersonal responsibilities—between family and friends, but also between strangers—shared among a society bound by its traumas.
“My Name Is Lucy Barton” (2016) introduced the preternaturally sensitive narrator who grew up amid poverty and abuse in rural Illinois and went on to write successful books about her childhood and life in its shadow. The mysteries of memory and perspective come to the fore in the linked stories in “Anything Is Possible” (2017), involving the people Lucy left behind in her hometown, who have decidedly mixed feelings about her interpretation of the past. Lucy narrates again in “Oh William!,” and while her own grief and fears are carefully considered, the novel’s focus is on the psychological crisis that assails her aging and lonely ex-husband.

The Urdu-language author published more than 20 collections of short stories. He also wrote screenplays for the Bombay film industry, radio plays and a ceaseless flow of journalism. Frequently peopled by pimps, prostitutes, hoodlums, gamblers and hard-drinking bohemians (like their creator), his fictional dispatches from the seamy side of midcentury South Asian societies saw him prosecuted for obscenity six times: three cases under the Raj in British India, three in newly independent Pakistan. Anti-Muslim rioting in Bombay, the city he loved and chronicled, prompted his family to move north to Lahore after the Partition of India in 1947.
An endlessly fertile storyteller, Manto charmed even his accusers. He once befriended the Pakistani judge who had just fined him for a scandalous tale. “Society itself is obscene,” he told the judge. “All I do is represent its essence. It is entirely understandable that people with ugly faces vent their anger at the mirror.”
